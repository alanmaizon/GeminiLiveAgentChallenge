This is Logos — a realtime Ancient Greek scholarly console built with Gemini Live.

We open on the welcome screen. Before starting a session, a passage has been pinned
for close reading. The text is Pindar's Pythian Eight — skias onar anthropos —
man is a dream of a shadow. This is the close-reading feature: a passage pinned at
the top stays visible above the conversation for the entire session.

The difficulty is set to Beginner. We start the first session.

The first demonstration is structured morphology. I type: "Parse anthropos for me" —
asking Logos to analyze the word that appears in the pinned passage above.

The response streams in live. As Logos speaks, it invokes parse underscore greek —
a declared tool that returns the lemma, part of speech, grammatical case, gender,
number, and definition. The result renders as a card directly in the transcript.

The word is anthropos — second declension, masculine, nominative singular.
Lemma: anthropos. Meaning: human being, person. The token count increments in the
corner of the inspector panel in real time.

The inspector drawer is open on the right. It shows the raw tool dot call event:
the function name and the arguments passed to it. Then the tool dot result flowing
back through the WebSocket. This is not interpretation. It is a structured output
from a declared tool, visible in full.

The session ends. The passage is cleared from the transcript. The difficulty badge
transitions from Beginner to Intermediate.

In the second segment, Logos is shown an image — a page from an illustrated edition
of Aesop's fable of the Crow and the Fox, in Ancient Greek. The image is injected
as a base64 frame over the same WebSocket connection that carries voice and text.

I then ask: "Look up korax in the lexicon" — korax, the crow, the first word of the
Aesop fable. Logos invokes lookup underscore lexicon.

The result is a full LSJ-style entry. Definitions numbered in order of attestation.
Usage notes. Key textual references. This is not a paraphrase or a summary — it is
a structured lexicon card with principal parts, numbered senses, and citations,
rendered directly in the transcript.

The session ends. Difficulty transitions from Intermediate to Advanced.

The third segment is metrical scansion. I give Logos the opening line of the Iliad —
Menin aeide thea, Peleiadeo Achileos — the first line of Western epic literature.

Logos calls scan underscore meter. The response returns the complete dactylic
hexameter pattern: each syllable marked long or short, the feet labeled one by one,
and a note on the spondaic opening characteristic of Homeric verse. The pattern
renders in the inspector font — long strokes and cups — foot by foot across the line.

Each session in this demo resets cleanly between segments. One browser context.
One page. Three tools. Three difficulty levels. The workflow is deliberate,
repeatable, and presentation-ready.

Logos is not a general-purpose chatbot. It is a tool-grounded scholarly console
with structured outputs, visible reasoning surfaces, and a clear separation between
spoken interaction and visual analysis.

The interface supports voice, camera, close reading of pinned passages, and adaptive
difficulty from beginner through advanced — designed for serious engagement with
Ancient Greek texts.

Thank you for watching.
